### Notes

First exercise of the design thinking workshop. The goal: list real problems I've personally hit, then pick a few and stress-test them with a small structured frame.

I started with four problems off the top of my head:

- Communicating between AIs. Right now, when I want one model to weigh in on what another is doing, I'm literally pasting questions back and forth between VS Code (Gemini) and a Claude tab. No shared state, no context, just copy/paste.
- AI not correctly seeing what's on the screen. Especially front-end work, where a model claims it understands the rendered output but is actually just reading the source.
- Streaming and music not conforming to what you want to hear. The platform doesn't reliably serve a specific mood. The example I used was NBA games on Prime, where the experience around the game (and the recs after) feels untuned.
- People coming home from jail not being able to find apartments that are welcoming.
- Finding law offices to advertise Westlaw products to. (Day job context. Identifying which firms are worth pitching is harder than it sounds.)

Then I picked three to flesh out with the Who / How often / Current workaround frame:

**Communicating between AIs.**
- Who: developers using more than one model in the same workflow
- How often: daily
- Current workaround: forcing one AI to generate a "state document" before switching platforms

**AI not correctly seeing what's on the screen.**
- Who: front-end developers
- How often: often, mid-task
- Current workaround: live AI screen streaming, which is partial and slow

**Finding law offices to advertise Westlaw products to.**
- Who: legal tech sales teams
- How often: daily
- Current workaround: intent-based data platforms (expensive and noisy)

### What I learned

The Who / How often / Workaround frame is a sneaky-good filter. It immediately separated the problems I actually feel from the problems that just sounded interesting. NBA on Prime is a complaint, not a problem worth building for. The other three each had a real "who" and a real "how often", which is what made the legal tech one the obvious pick later.
