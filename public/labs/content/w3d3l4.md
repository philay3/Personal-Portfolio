### Notes

Exercise 4 was the ship-and-tell moment. Posted the live URL in three different places to three different audiences, each with a different ask.

### The three places

- Class Slack channel. Shared `philanthony.dev` with a one-line note about what I built. Visited classmates' sites in turn and left short reactions on three of them. This is the easiest one because the audience is in the same boat as me.
- A friend outside the class. Asked them to open the link on their phone and tell me whether anything looked broken. Reply: "Looks clean. Nav doesn't feel like a wall of links." That was the feedback I most wanted, because the nav was something I'd specifically iterated on. Friend-outside-the-class is the test for whether the site reads as a real thing to someone who doesn't know the context.
- LinkedIn. Posted a short update with the URL and a one-paragraph reflection on building with AI as a collaborator. Got a few new connections from other Next Chapter cohort members and some IT folks from my old network.

### What I learned

Sharing felt vulnerable the first time, less so the second, and pretty normal by the third. The activation energy is in the first post. After that, each subsequent share is a copy-paste with a tweaked sentence.

The lab is right that this is a skill in its own right. Building the site is one kind of work. Telling people the site exists is a separate kind of work, and you can be bad at one and good at the other. I'd been treating shipping as the end. The share is the actual end.
