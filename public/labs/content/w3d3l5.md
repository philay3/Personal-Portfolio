### Notes

Day 4 is the Phase 1 competency gate: 3 to 5 minutes, live demo plus code walkthrough plus reflection. Exercise 5 was writing the script, practicing it once out loud, timing the run, and trimming where I went over.

### Script structure

Four blocks, mapped to time targets.

### What is it (30 sec)

> I built philanthony.dev, my personal portfolio. It's a single-page site aimed at future employers and bootcamp peers, showing who I am as a software engineer in the Next Chapter program, what I've built, and what I'm writing about. Let me show you the live site.

(Open philanthony.dev. Scroll top to bottom, surfacing each section.)

### Code walkthrough (1-2 min)

> Here's the codebase. I want to walk through the scroll spy because it's the most interesting decision in the project. As you scroll, the nav link for the current section lights up. It's built on the Intersection Observer API.

(Show `script.js` in VS Code.)

> I create a single observer that watches every section. When a section enters the viewport, the observer fires a callback, and I find the nav link with the matching href and add an `is-active` class to it. The CSS handles the styling.
>
> The tricky part was the rootMargin setting. Without it, two sections were getting marked "in view" at the same time whenever the viewport could show both. Tightening rootMargin to negative 40 percent on top and bottom shrinks the observer's active zone to a horizontal band in the middle of the viewport. Now only one section is active at a time.

### AI collaboration (1-2 min)

> AI scaffolded the initial HTML structure. I gave it a specific prompt with my wireframe and section names, and it produced semantic HTML with the right class names. What I changed: every word of placeholder copy got rewritten in my voice, the contact form section got deleted because it was out of scope, and the class names got renamed from generic numbered ones to descriptive ones.
>
> Where I overrode AI: the first scroll spy attempt AI suggested used a scroll event listener with `getBoundingClientRect` math. That works, but it fires on every scroll pixel and needs throttling. I asked for an Intersection Observer version instead because it's the modern, performant pattern. AI gave me one that highlighted multiple links at once. I diagnosed and fixed that myself with the rootMargin trick rather than going back to ask.
>
> The general rule I landed on this week: AI generates structure. I generate voice and judgment. AI defaults to the fanciest tool it knows: `reduce`, spread, `transition: all`, eight media queries. My job is keeping what's clearer for me and trimming the flex.

### What I learned (30 sec)

> The biggest thing I learned this week is that scoping ruthlessly on Day 1 is what made shipping on Day 3 possible. Every nice-to-have I cut on Day 1 was a day I didn't burn later. Gallery is still empty and that's fine, because it was always nice-to-have. Working from a scope doc kept me building instead of debating.

### What I learned

Practicing out loud is non-negotiable. The script that reads at 4 minutes silently runs at 5+ minutes out loud, every time, because of pauses, breathing, finding the right window in VS Code, and the half-second where you realize you've said the same word three times. The only way to know your real timing is to run it.

Trimming is where the talk gets sharp. The first draft is what you'd say if no one had a deadline. The trimmed version is what you'd say if it actually mattered. Both versions are technically "your talk." Only one is the one to give.
